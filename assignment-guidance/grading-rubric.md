# Grading Criteria for Writing Assignments

**Professor Jayme R. Schlesinger — Introduction to International Politics (POLS UN1601)**

## What Earns an A (93-100)

1. **In-depth, thoughtfully considered responses** to each question
2. **Clear and cohesive argument** for each question — not summary, but analysis
3. **Incorporates relevant course materials** into the discussion substantively
4. **Properly cited throughout** — every reference to a reading must be cited
5. **Structured properly** — intro paragraph, body discussion, concluding paragraph
6. **Clean writing** — no significant grammatical errors, awkward writing, or typos

## What Drops to A- (90-92)

- Meets most A requirements but has a minor issue
- Argument is clear but **not as well expressed** due to grammar or awkward writing
- OR a **small logical error or error of interpretation** that doesn't derail the response

## What Drops to B+ (87-89)

Missing one or two A-level elements:
- **Too much summary, not enough argument** — demonstrates understanding but lacks independent thought
- OR has thoughtful arguments but **doesn't interact enough with the readings**
- OR clear arguments with readings but **significant logic issues**
- OR all of the above but **noticeable grammar/writing problems** that hurt clarity

## What Drops to B (83-86)

Missing several elements:
- Well-written but **lacks thoughtful engagement with theories and materials**
- OR demonstrates effort but **struggling with the course materials**

## What Drops to B- (80-82)

- Fulfills basic requirements but **lacks substantive discussion**
- Poorer writing quality — not reviewed for errors before submitting
- **Missing citations** where materials should be cited

## What Drops to C (70-79)

- Does not fulfill basic requirements
- Missing responses to questions, or under minimum word count, or repetitive filler

## F (0)

- Not submitted, plagiarized, or academic honesty violation

---

## Key Takeaways for A-Grade Work

1. **Answer the question directly** — don't just summarize readings
2. **Make an argument** — have a thesis, defend it with evidence from course materials
3. **Cite everything** — every time you reference a reading, cite it
4. **Use multiple sources** — incorporate various course materials, not just one or two
5. **Structure matters** — intro → argument → conclusion, with clear paragraph logic
6. **Edit before submitting** — clean, polished writing free of errors
