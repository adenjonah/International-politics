# Diversionary Aggression in Chinese Foreign Policy

**By Erin Baggott Carter**

## Introduction

In 2018, Xi Jinping broke with established norms by positioning himself to rule indefinitely, generating opposition among the ruling elite who were already angered by his anti-corruption initiatives. Carter argues that Xi confronts significant threats and will therefore pursue diversionary foreign policy -- initiating international conflicts to cultivate nationalism and protect himself from elite conspiracies.

The author contends that "China's domestic politics compel risky behavior on the international stage." Research suggests diversionary aggression may account for approximately 40 percent of conflicts China initiates with the United States.

## The Reasoning Behind Diversionary Aggression

Chinese politics are characterized by competitive factional struggles. Xi needs to insulate himself from regime insiders through mechanisms that build popular support.

Following Machiavelli's observation, autocrats can leverage public backing against elite conspiracies. The Chinese population is notably nationalist, expressing strong support for the government during international disputes. When citizens perceive Xi as defending national interests, they become reluctant to support efforts to remove him.

International crises enable leaders to demonstrate competence and activate nationalist solidarity, thereby securing domestic protection against dissatisfied elites who recognize that overthrowing a popular leader invites public backlash.

## Measuring Diversionary Aggression

The Shanghai Stock Exchange (SSE) serves as a patronage mechanism. Elite officials gain enormous wealth through pre-IPO share purchases at undervalued prices. When SSE performance declines, elite financial interests suffer, creating incentives for opposition to Xi.

Carter's research covering 1990-2010 analyzed over 3,000 bilateral US-China interactions. The findings demonstrate that when the SSE drops 5-15 percent -- volatility occurring three times monthly -- the Chinese government doubles its likelihood of initiating disputes with the United States.

Supporting evidence includes:

**Propaganda patterns:** When the SSE declines, state media increases positive coverage of "party-mass relations," signaling to elites their broad public support.

**Diplomatic reversals:** China quadruples diplomatic engagement following diversionary episodes, typically through private channels, suggesting aggression is tactical rather than strategic.

## The 2010 Case Study

Between January and July 2010, the SSE lost one-third of its value. During this crisis:

- China presented lengthy statements about South China Sea territorial claims as a "national priority"
- State media reported bilateral relations were "strained" and "at a low point"
- The Global Times editor told US Ambassador John Huntsman that belligerence was "necessary to satisfy the Chinese people"

Once the SSE stabilized in July, China reversed course. Officials accepted President Hu's Washington visit, supported UN Security Council statements on North Korea, reauthorized military exchanges, and clarified that the South China Sea was not officially designated a "core interest."

Despite saber-rattling, "Beijing's diversion left no lasting imprint upon the international order."

## Policy Implications for the United States

The US faces a strategic dilemma distinguishing between diversionary and revisionist aggression:

- **False positives** (treating diversion as revision): Risk involves China exploiting restraint to expand regional influence
- **False negatives** (treating revision as diversion): Risk involves mounting containment responses during diversionary episodes, antagonizing Xi precisely when he needs to appear tough, potentially triggering escalatory spirals neither side intended

False negatives present greater danger. China consistently withdraws diversionary claims after domestic crises pass. Security dilemmas, once activated, prove difficult to deescalate.

The US operates from preponderant power. If Xi exploits American restraint, Washington can restore the status quo relatively quickly. If Xi refuses to withdraw claims after domestic tensions ease, this signals genuine revisionist intentions.

Therefore, the US should respond to diversionary aggression with strategic restraint and reserve containment for genuine revisionism.

## Identifying Diversionary Aggression

Four distinguishing principles:

1. Diversionary aggression occurs only amid serious Xi-elite tensions. Without such tensions, treat aggression as revisionism.

2. Accompanying propaganda emphasizing Xi's popularity indicates diversionary intent.

3. Public aggression observable to Chinese citizens suggests diversion, while private military actions suggest genuine power-balance revision attempts.

4. Diversionary aggression ceases when domestic crises pass, followed by sustained private diplomacy and potential cooperation on issues kept confidential. Absence of these indicators suggests actual revisionism.
