# Sovereignty and Non-Intervention: China Deserts its Principles on Ukraine

**By Thomas Eder** -- April 1, 2022

## Introduction

Despite consistently championing sovereignty, territorial integrity, and non-interference, China has refused to condemn Russia's February 24, 2022 invasion of Ukraine. The author argues this represents an abandonment of stated principles in favor of geopolitical interests.

## China's Historical Stance on Sovereignty and Intervention

China has maintained a longstanding position rejecting military interventions lacking UN Security Council authorization. Following the 1989 Tiananmen incident, Beijing shifted from absolute sovereignty positions to selectively support certain UN-sanctioned interventions during the 1990s -- while continuing to veto others it opposed.

## The Five Principles of Peaceful Co-existence

Before joining the UN in 1971, China and India championed the "Five Principles of Peaceful Co-existence," emphasizing "respect for others' sovereignty, non-aggression, and...non-interference in other's internal affairs."

Russia's demands that Ukraine alter its constitution and accept a Russian-appointed prime minister directly violate both letter and spirit of these principles.

## Domestic Sensitivities: Taiwan and Xinjiang

Territorial integrity represents China's paramount objective. Beijing fears separatism and foreign interference regarding Xinjiang and Taiwan.

## The Strategic Partnership

The geopolitically crucial Russia-China relationship, described as having "no limits," complicates principle-based positions. Benefits include strategic security, diplomatic support against US pressure, ideological alignment on authoritarian governance, and energy security cooperation.

## Conclusion

Geopolitical interests have superseded international law principles. China presents itself as supporting sovereignty while defending Russia's actions -- revealing selective application serving strategic partnerships over consistent legal frameworks.
