# The Trajectory of Chinese Foreign Policy: From Reactive Assertiveness to Opportunistic Activism

**By Ryan Hass** — November 4, 2017

## Overview

China perceives its ascendancy as a return to its historical role as a dominant global power. The commentary argues that rather than representing a sudden shift under Xi Jinping's leadership, China's increasingly assertive foreign policy reflects a long-standing narrative about restoring China's position following what Beijing characterizes as a period of national humiliation.

## China's Historical Narrative

The Chinese government frames its rise along "a continuum that eventually returns it to the center of East Asia and a restoration of its role as a leading global power." Chinese leadership views the period beginning with the Opium War (1840-42) as an aberration in an otherwise glorious history. The Communist Party has consistently emphasized that "the West and Japan are determined to keep China down, and that only the Communist Party is strong enough to guide China" toward recovery.

## Xi Jinping's Vision

Under Xi's leadership, this narrative has gained renewed momentum. He has mobilized the population toward the "great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation" and established two major "centenary goals": achieving a moderately prosperous society by 2021 (the Communist Party's hundredth anniversary) and becoming a prosperous, strong, advanced nation by 2049 (the People's Republic's centenary).

## Western Perspective vs. Chinese Self-Perception

Many Western observers characterize China's behavior as increasingly "assertive, even revisionist," viewing it as a challenge to the post-World War II international order. However, China's own perspective differs fundamentally -- Beijing sees its rise as restoring the "natural order" rather than challenging it.
