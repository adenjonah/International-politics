# How Does the European Union Work?

**By James McBride** — Updated March 11, 2022

## Introduction

The European Union represents a remarkable experiment in regional integration spanning the post-World War II era. Member nations have progressively transferred decision-making authority to shared institutions pursuing peace and economic growth. The bloc has confronted significant challenges recently, including financial crises, migration pressures, Brexit, and responses to Russian aggression, particularly following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

## Main EU Institutions

Seven official institutions form the foundation of EU governance:

**European Council**: Comprises heads of state and government, setting broad strategic direction through summits.

**European Commission**: The executive branch proposing legislation, managing budgets, and representing the EU internationally.

**European Parliament**: The sole directly elected body where representatives from each member state participate in co-decision with other institutions on legislation and budgetary matters.

**Council of the European Union**: Ministers from member states organized by policy area, providing required legislative approval alongside Parliament.

**Court of Justice**: The highest judicial authority interpreting EU law and resolving disputes between member states and institutions.

**European Central Bank**: Managing the euro currency for nineteen member states and implementing monetary policy.

**European Court of Auditors**: Responsible for financial oversight and fraud detection.

## How Institutions Relate

Democratic legitimacy flows from national elections and direct parliamentary elections. The Council and Parliament jointly determine Commission membership. All legislation requires both Parliament and Council approval under a co-decision arrangement.

## EU Legal Authority

Member states delegated varying levels of authority across three categories:

**Exclusive competencies** limit lawmaking to the EU alone in areas including customs, competition rules, trade, and eurozone monetary policy.

**Shared competencies** allow national legislation unless EU law already addresses the matter, covering the single market, agriculture, regional development, transportation, energy, environmental protection, and research.

**Supporting competencies** restrict EU involvement to bolstering member state activities in culture, education, sports, and social policies.

## Foreign Policy Framework

The Common Foreign and Security Policy depends on unanimous member state agreement, meaning individual nations retain independent foreign policy authority. Russia's 2022 Ukraine invasion prompted Germany to reverse military spending policy and intensified EU sanctions against Russian entities.

## Trade Policy Mechanisms

Unlike broader foreign policy, trade policy transferred entirely to EU control through the Common Commercial Policy. Unified tariffs and single-market rules necessitate collective negotiation.

## Military Capabilities

No independent EU army exists. NATO remains Europe's primary collective defense mechanism. Russia's Ukraine invasion accelerated defense discussions, with Germany doubling military spending commitments while the EU financed weapons transfers to Ukraine.

## Budget Scope and Scale

EU spending totaled approximately $197 billion in 2020:
- Agriculture programs (35%)
- Economic and social cohesion (35%)
- Competitiveness initiatives (14%)
- Global Europe and security/citizenship (remaining)
