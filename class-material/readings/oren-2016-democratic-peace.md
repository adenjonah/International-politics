# Democratic Peace

**Author:** Ido Oren
**Fact-checked by:** Britannica Editors

## Overview

Democratic peace refers to the proposition that "democratic states never (or almost never) wage war on one another." This concept differs from claims about democracies being generally more peaceful -- scholars widely accept the former while debating the latter.

## Historical Origins

The theory traces back to German philosopher Immanuel Kant's *Project for a Perpetual Peace* (1795), which envisioned establishing "a zone of peace among states constituted as republics." U.S. President Woodrow Wilson echoed these ideas in 1917, declaring the United States aimed to make the world "safe for democracy."

Contemporary scholars often use the terms "democratic peace," "liberal peace," and "Kantian peace" interchangeably, noting that Kant's republicanism aligns with modern liberal democracy concepts.

## Modern Research Development

International relations scholar Michael Doyle revived Kant's work through influential mid-1980s articles, arguing that Kant's envisioned peace zone had gradually materialized. Following the Cold War's end, democratic peace became a major research focus, with quantitative studies demonstrating that while interstate wars are rare generally, "wars between democracies have been even rarer."

## Competing Explanations

Scholars debate *why* democratic peace exists, offering two primary theories:

**Cultural Explanation:** Democratic societies share norms favoring peaceful dispute resolution. Citizens apply these peaceful expectations to other democracies, making leaders reluctant to pursue violent conflict.

**Structural Explanation:** Democratic institutions -- separation of powers and checks and balances -- constrain leaders' ability to initiate war rashly. The deliberative decision-making process allows diplomats time for peaceful resolution.

## Theoretical Context

Democratic peace aligns with liberal international relations theory, which also emphasizes peace through economic interdependence and international institutions. This contrasts with realism, which argues that anarchic international structures -- regardless of regime type -- perpetuate potential violence.

## Policy Applications

The concept influenced U.S. foreign policy significantly. President Bill Clinton's 1990s administration justified spreading democracy globally using democratic peace arguments. Similarly, neoconservative officials cited democratic peace theory to support military intervention in Iraq, believing democratization would create regional stability.
