# Rally 'Round the Flag: US Opinion Pre and Post 9/11

## Public Opinion before the War

Iraq dominated headlines throughout fall 2002 and early 2003. From August 2002 through March 2003, support for military action remained relatively stable, hovering between 52-59 percent in favor, while opposition ranged from 35-43 percent.

Support divided sharply along partisan lines. Republicans backed the war at 75 percent, compared to just 40 percent of Democrats. Age also mattered -- six of ten Americans aged 18-29 supported invasion, versus fewer than five of ten Americans over 65.

Demographic opposition was limited. Black Americans, those with postgraduate education, and Democrats each showed majority opposition. Interestingly, women (51 percent) and Hispanic Americans (60 percent) largely supported military action.

Notably, even those favoring war weren't demanding it. When asked if they'd be upset if Bush decided against war, roughly half said no. Support was "permissive -- it was willing to follow the White House to war but not demanding war."

## The Rally Effect

As diplomacy collapsed in mid-March, support jumped. The final pre-invasion Gallup poll showed 64 percent approval. Once Operation Iraqi Freedom began on March 19, support surged to 72 percent and remained there throughout fighting.

However, Bush's personal approval gains were modest -- a 13-percentage-point rise -- much smaller than his 35-point jump after September 11 or his father's 24-point increase during the 1991 Gulf War.

The modest rally reflected deep partisan divisions. Over 90 percent of Republicans supported the war, compared to only half of Democrats. Racial disparities also emerged: 78 percent of whites supported the invasion, but only 29 percent of Black Americans did -- a striking contrast to the 1991 Gulf War.

Patriotic sentiment extended beyond Bush himself. Congressional approval jumped 7 points to 52 percent. Public satisfaction with the country's direction surged from 36 to 60 percent.

## Early Evaluations

In late April, 70 percent believed Bush was right to order the attack. Most Americans remained optimistic, with over 80 percent saying things were going "very well" or "moderately well."

Confidence in the war on terrorism surged to 65 percent (up from 37 percent pre-invasion). When specifically asked about safety, 58 percent said the war made Americans safer.

Regarding weapons of mass destruction, the public proved forgiving. In early April, 58 percent said the war would be justified even without finding such weapons -- a dramatic shift from the 38 percent who held that view at fighting's start.

Americans showed patience with reconstruction timelines. Three-to-one majorities rejected quick withdrawal, instead supporting efforts to establish democracy even if requiring year-plus occupation. Most expected occupation lasting at least six months.

One notable departure: 52 percent favored UN oversight of Iraq's transition government, with 65 percent supporting UN-led humanitarian assistance -- likely reflecting pragmatic burden-sharing desires rather than ideological commitment.

## Domestic Political Implications

Historical precedent suggested Bush couldn't translate military victory into domestic policy gains. His father failed after the 1991 Gulf War. The younger Bush experienced similar obstacles following September 11 and Afghanistan operations -- Democrats blocked stimulus proposals, rejected Arctic drilling, and stonewalled judicial nominees despite military successes.

Even mid-war, Congress proved defiant domestically. The Republican-controlled Senate killed the Arctic drilling proposal on the war's second day and cut Bush's $726 billion tax cut in half.

Looking toward 2004, vulnerabilities emerged. In late April, 54 percent said Bush wasn't paying enough attention to the economy. Americans opposed his tax cuts by 47-42 percent, and were evenly split on whether he was "in touch" with ordinary Americans' problems.

## Historical Context

Public evaluations of war legitimacy shift over time. After the 1991 Gulf War, 72 percent thought Kuwait's liberation was worthwhile. A decade later, with Saddam still in power, only 51 percent maintained that view.

The analysis notes that public support for interventions (like Lebanon in the 1980s and Somalia in the 1990s) can collapse rapidly following deadly attacks on American forces, potentially producing intense withdrawal pressure.
