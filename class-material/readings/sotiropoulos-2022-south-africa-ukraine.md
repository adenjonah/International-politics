# How do Global South politics of non-alignment and solidarity explain South Africa's position on Ukraine?

**By Elizabeth Sidiropoulos** — August 2, 2022

## Introduction

The Russian invasion of Ukraine represents a pivotal moment for international relations, particularly within the Global South. While Western analysts view this conflict as a watershed event reshaping the post-Cold War order, many developing nations see it as an opportunity to reassert foreign policy independence and non-alignment principles as geopolitical tensions intensify between competing powers.

## South Africa's Contradictory Response

South Africa's initial reaction demonstrated the complexity of Global South positioning. The Foreign Ministry initially called for Russian withdrawal, then reversed course within days. This was followed by abstention at the United Nations General Assembly and advocacy for bilateral negotiations between the parties involved.

Understanding this apparent inconsistency requires examining two key factors: South Africa's established foreign policy doctrine and its commitment to solidarity with historical allies.

## Foreign Policy Principles and Positions

### Non-Alignment Framework

South Africa maintains what officials describe as an "independent and non-aligned foreign policy" explicitly designed to avoid entanglement in great power conflicts. The government characterizes the Ukraine conflict not as Russia versus Ukraine, but as a proxy struggle between Russia and NATO, suggesting NATO's eastward expansion created legitimate Russian security concerns.

### Commitment to Peaceful Resolution

Peaceful dispute resolution has anchored South African foreign policy since 1994. The nation has mediated various African conflicts -- including those in the DRC, Burundi, and South Sudan.

### Opposition to Unilateral Sanctions

South Africa generally opposes Western-imposed unilateral sanctions, viewing them as reflecting double standards in addressing different conflicts.

## Politics of Solidarity

### Historical Context

The ruling African National Congress government bases foreign policy significantly on solidarity with parties and nations supporting liberation struggles against apartheid. The ANC's lengthy relationship with the Soviet Union -- which supported armed resistance and educated many ANC leaders -- contrasts sharply with U.S. opposition during the 1980s, when American and British administrations labeled the ANC terrorist.

### BRICS Dimension

BRICS membership adds another solidarity layer. For South Africa, the BRICS consortium permits engagement with rising superpower China and other influential Global South leaders sharing views on reforming the international system.

## Conclusion

Global South nations increasingly resist automatic alignment when great powers demand support. South Africa's position reflects this broader shift: developing democracies assess entire historical records when determining geopolitical alignment. The Ukraine invasion demonstrated that Western nations cannot assume developing country support, necessitating more nuanced approaches to international persuasion and alliance-building.
