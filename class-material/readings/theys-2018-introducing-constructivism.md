# Introducing Constructivism in International Relations Theory

**By Sarina Theys**

## Introduction

Constructivism emerged in International Relations theory partly due to traditional theories' inability to explain the Cold War's end. Realism and liberalism, with their focus on state power competition, failed to account for how ordinary people's actions drove this historic shift. Constructivism addresses this gap by proposing that "the social world is of our making" and that actors continually shape international relations through their interactions.

## The Basics of Constructivism

### Social Construction of Reality

Constructivism posits that both the world and our knowledge of it are socially constructed. Alexander Wendt's famous example illustrates this: "500 British nuclear weapons are less threatening to the United States than five North Korean nuclear weapons." The difference lies not in material weapons but in the meanings assigned to them -- the ideational structures that matter more than material ones.

Nuclear weapons themselves carry no inherent meaning; context determines their significance. This demonstrates that constructivists examine how ideas and beliefs shape international politics beyond mere material conditions.

### Agency and Structure

Constructivists argue that agency and structure mutually constitute each other. Structure includes both material and ideational elements of the international system, while agency refers to actors' capacity to act. This differs fundamentally from realism, which claims that anarchic structures determine state behavior.

Wendt's assertion that "anarchy is what states make of it" encapsulates this view. States can interpret anarchy differently based on the meanings they assign to it, opening possibilities for change.

### Identities and Interests

States possess multiple socially constructed identities that signal their interests. These identities shape what states consider appropriate actions. For instance, a small state's identity implies survival-focused interests, while a great power's identity suggests global dominance concerns.

Germany illustrates this principle. Despite becoming an economic powerhouse post-1945, Germany adopted a pacifist rather than militarist identity following Nazi atrocities, demonstrating how historical experience reshapes state identity.

### Social Norms

Social norms function as standards for appropriate behavior among actors with particular identities. Three types exist:

- **Regulative norms**: Order and constrain behavior
- **Constitutive norms**: Create new actors, interests, or action categories
- **Prescriptive norms**: Prescribe certain behaviors as correct

Norms undergo a lifecycle: emergence, acceptance, and internalization. Climate change mitigation exemplifies this process -- decades of advocacy transformed environmental protection into expected state behavior.

### Variations Within Constructivism

**Conventional constructivists** ask "what" questions, seeking causal explanations for actor behavior. They believe identities can predict when they become visible.

**Critical constructivists** ask "how" questions about identity formation, emphasizing language's role in constructing social reality through communication.

Most constructivists position themselves between these extremes.

## Constructivism Applied: Bhutan's National Interests

### Material and Ideational Structures

Bhutan, a small Buddhist kingdom with 745,000 people and 38,394 square kilometers, faces geopolitical constraints. It borders China and India, two rival major powers, and China disputes Bhutanese territory.

From a realist perspective, Bhutan's small size and geographic vulnerability should severely limit its sovereignty. Constructivism offers a different interpretation: material constraints don't solely determine outcomes. The meanings Bhutan assigns to these conditions matter profoundly.

When China annexed Tibet in the 1950s, Bhutan perceived threat and closed its northern border, turning toward India. This created an ideational structure of enmity with China and friendship with India -- social relationships subject to change should beliefs shift.

### National Identity Construction

Bhutan developed a distinctive identity as "the last surviving independent Mahayana Buddhist Kingdom in the world." This identity emerged through a deliberate "Bhutanisation" process beginning in the 1980s under the fourth king.

The **Driglam Namzhag** (code of conduct) represents a regulative norm mandating:
- Observance of vows emphasizing kinship loyalty and respect
- Wearing national dress (gho for men, kira for women)
- Dzongkha as the national language

This policy created cultural unity despite Bhutan's multi-ethnic, multi-religious, multi-lingual population. The Lhotshampas (Nepali-descended Hindus) faced severe consequences, including expulsion in the 1990s when authorities reclassified them as non-nationals.

### The Norm Lifecycle in Action

The Driglam Namzhag progressed through three phases:

1. **Norm emergence**: Authorities created the code
2. **Norm acceptance**: Citizens adopted it, including national dress and language
3. **Norm internalization**: Behavior became circumscribed by these norms

This constitutive process created "Bhutanese citizens" who act according to specific rules. These norms remain regulated today -- citizens must wear national dress during national events, school, and work.

### Alternative Identities and Gross National Happiness

Bhutanese elites constructed a second identity: leaders in holistic, sustainable development. This identity rests on **Gross National Happiness (GNH)**, which criticizes GDP for ignoring spiritual wellbeing and promotes balance between material prosperity and mental peace.

Using the United Nations as a platform, Bhutanese leaders promoted GNH internationally. The UN subsequently adopted Resolution 65/309, recognizing that pursuing happiness constitutes a fundamental goal and that GDP inadequately measures wellbeing.

This second identity allows Bhutan to signal sovereign independence and increase international visibility -- advantageous when regional tensions escalate.

## Conclusion

Constructivism appears obvious: actions, interactions, and perceptions shape reality. Yet this insight carries significant theoretical implications. The discipline benefits from constructivism's attention to issues mainstream theories neglect, particularly realism's oversight of ideational factors.

Constructivists demonstrate that state behavior reflects not only material power distribution and geography but also ideas, identities, and norms. By emphasizing ideational factors, constructivists reveal that international reality isn't fixed -- it remains subject to change through actors' reconceptualization of meaning and identity.
