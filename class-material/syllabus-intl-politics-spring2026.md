## Course Description

Why do countries go to war? How do alliances function? How do histories of conflict shape conflict in the current system? Why do states open their borders to trade? Does the economic interdependence strengthen cultural ties between countries? Can multi-national corporations (MNCs) shape international politics? How do social movements influence international politics? What are international organizations, and what is the role of international organizations such as the World Trade Organization (WTO) or the United States (UN) in international politics? Does UN peacekeeping work? What is International Humanitarian Law (IHL), and how relevant is IHL to international politics today? What causes civil war? How do countries address the threat of terrorism? What shaped countries' responses to issues such as the COVID-19 pandemic? What conditions would foster greater cooperation on climate change?

Students will consider these questions in this course. In our lectures, we will review the theories developed to address patterns of conflict and cooperation in the international system and use these theories to examine international politics since the twentieth century. We will consider the causes of events in international politics, such as the end of the Cold War, the development of the international political economy (IPE), the rise of China in the international system, the Russian War in Ukraine, terrorism and states' responses, the emergence of COVID-19 and its consequences, changes to U.S. tariff policies, and Iran's nuclear weapons development. Students will be expected to engage in discussion of these events in their discussion sections (which students must register for in addition to the lecture) and in a series of written assignments. By the end of this course, students will be able to demonstrate a broad factual and causal knowledge of significant events in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, as well as an ability to analyze the causes of these events using the theories reviewed. Students will also be able to demonstrate an ability to distinguish between the foundations of these theories to assess to utility of these theories in practical application to current events as they unfold.

## Required Readings

Jack S. Levy and William R. Thompson. Causes of War. (Oxford: Wiley-Blackwell, 2010)

All readings not included in Levy and Thompson (2010) will be made available through Courseworks or can be accessed through the Columbia Library or Open Web.

Students are encouraged to stay up-to-date with the readings assigned each week. The course is designed to build on the previous readings and lectures. Students may read ahead at their own pace. However, it is more important that students have managed their time to read what is assigned on a given week and that students do not skip the readings for any week.

The readings included in this course were chosen because their relevance to current debates in the literature and are not to be misinterpreted as the only available resource for insight into the relevant literature. Students are encouraged to think critically about the material, in particular during discussion sections and in their written assignments.

Some readings may be longer than others or more difficult to complete. Students are encouraged to read mindfully as they work through the course material. Students should not attempt to read "every word" of the assigned readings, but rather work through the readings in a way that they are able to understand the core arguments presented in the readings and can discuss the readings in their discussion sections.

## Course Requirements

### Section Attendance and Participation

Attendance and participation are important parts of study in political science. Participation in weekly discussion sections is required through enrollment in the linked UN1611 (non-credit) course. Students are expected to contribute substantively to discussion sections. Participation may take on various forms including asking questions that are relevant to the course materials and actively participating in discussion activities.

Students who expect to be absent from their discussion sections for religious observance (holiday), illness, or other emergency are asked to notify the TA in charge of their discussion section. Students will be assigned a one-page essay make-up assignment by their TA to substitute for class participation on the day(s) missed.

### Written Assignments

There are three written assignments in this course. The purpose of these assignments is to encourage students to think critically about the course materials and to generate arguments regarding historical or current events in international politics with the course materials as a foundation. Assignment prompts will be posted two weeks prior to their submission deadline.

Assignments will be graded based on students' abilities to answer all questions with coherent and thoughtful discussion of the course materials. Improvement in students' writing is factored into the grading rules.

Late assignment submissions are subject to a late penalty of -5 points for submissions up to 48 hours passed the assignment deadline and -10 points for submissions up to 7 days passed the assignment deadline.

## Course Policies

### Academic Integrity

The intellectual venture in which we are all engaged requires of faculty and students alike the highest level of personal and academic integrity. As members of an academic community, each one of us bears the responsibility to participate in scholarly discourse and research in a manner characterized by intellectual honesty and scholarly integrity.

Scholarship, by its very nature, is an iterative process, with ideas and insights building one upon the other. Collaborative scholarship requires the study of other scholars' work, the free discussion of such work, and the explicit acknowledgement of those ideas in any work that inform our own. This exchange of ideas relies upon a mutual trust that sources, opinions, facts, and insights will be properly noted and carefully credited.

In practical terms, this means that, as students, you must be responsible for the full citations of others' ideas in all of your research papers and projects; you must be scrupulously honest when taking your examinations; you must always submit your own work and not that of another student, scholar, or internet agent.

### Plagiarism

All assignments for this course are to be completed in accordance with the Columbia College Honor Code. Assignments are to be submitted through Courseworks and will be processed through the TurnItIn system.

Students may work in groups to review the materials while brainstorming for their writing assignments. However, students are expected to work individually once their writing process begins.
