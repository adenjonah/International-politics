# Jonah Aden — Writing Style Guide

**Purpose:** When Claude writes academic prose for Jonah, follow these patterns exactly. The goal is prose that reads like Jonah wrote it, not like an LLM generated it.

---

## 1. Sentence-Level Patterns

### The "This shows/exemplifies" Analysis Sentence
Jonah's most distinctive habit. After presenting evidence, he interprets it with a sentence starting with "This":

- "This shows the state of Islam civilization in Al-Andalus in the 10th century, powerful and victorious."
- "This is the first example of political power changing hands in American cities."
- "This attitude exemplifies the struggle between reform movements and Political Machines."
- "This collection of political power into the hands of machines led to a growing frustration within the middle class."
- "This exclusion from machine style politics is what caused reformers to want to change the demographic."

**Rule:** After presenting a fact or quote, follow it with a "This [noun] [shows/exemplifies/demonstrates/is]..." sentence. This is how Jonah connects evidence to argument. Use this pattern frequently — it appears in nearly every paragraph.

### Long Compound Sentences with "and"
Jonah builds long sentences by chaining clauses with commas and "and":

- "The motivations for municipal reform were based on a feeling of exclusion from the governing structure of political machines and resulted in a desire to hold the political power by dismantling machine structure and implementing election changes that restricted the working class and ethnic minorities from the electorate and creating a new structure of municipal government to encourage efficiency, transparency, and responsiveness to the need of the upper and middle class."
- "Machines provided social services and encouraged democratic participation but their processes were corrupt and inefficient."

**Rule:** Don't break these into shorter sentences. Let them run. Jonah's sentences breathe through commas and conjunctions, not periods.

### "So," as a Casual Analytical Connector
Jonah starts sentences with "So," when drawing a conclusion mid-paragraph:

- "So, machine powers mobilized these groups intentionally..."
- "So, when PACs like Jerry Falwell's 'Moral Majority' rally around an issue like abortion, it is clearly important..."
- "So, although Smith would be correct that innovation would end under communism, this is the logical conclusion..."
- "So, they were largely inactive and dispersed for the next decade."
- "So, rather than acknowledge his flaws, she tries to write his practices off as a wash..."

**Rule:** Use "So," (with comma) to begin analytical sentences. This is Jonah's voice — conversational logic within formal writing.

### "In order to" Constructions
Jonah uses "In order to" frequently where most writers would just use "to":

- "In order for machines to reach this goal..."
- "In order to get the plurality of votes..."
- "In order to achieve a system with a strong central authority..."
- "...in order to ensure a more equitable form of government in urban America."

**Rule:** Use "In order to" rather than bare "to" for purpose clauses, especially at the start of sentences or when explaining motivations.

## 2. Transition and Connector Phrases

### Jonah's Go-To Transitions (USE THESE)
- **"As a result"** — his primary causal connector
- **"Moreover"** — for adding supporting points
- **"Additionally"** — same function as Moreover, alternated
- **"However"** — for counterpoints (not "Nevertheless" or "Nonetheless")
- **"To start"** — beginning a sequence of points
- **"Secondly"** — continuing a sequence
- **"For example"** — introducing illustrations
- **"While"** — for concessive clauses ("While reforms contributed to less corrupt governments, they have also...")
- **"Although"** — same concessive function, alternated with "While"
- **"Because of"** — starting sentences with causal explanation
- **"In the end"** — wrapping up a narrative arc within a section

### Transitions Jonah Does NOT Use (AVOID THESE)
- "Indeed" — never appears
- "Furthermore" — never appears (uses "Moreover" or "Additionally")
- "Nevertheless" / "Nonetheless" — uses "However" instead
- "Notably" / "It is worth noting" — never appears
- "Crucially" / "Importantly" — never appears
- "To be sure" — never appears
- "That said" — never appears
- "In essence" — never appears
- "Ultimately" — rarely (maybe once across all papers)
- "Put simply" / "Simply put" — never appears
- "It bears mentioning" — never appears
- "One might argue" — never appears (Jonah states things directly)

## 3. Paragraph and Argument Structure

### The Standard Jonah Paragraph
1. **Topic/claim sentence** — states the point directly
2. **Evidence** — facts, quotes, or historical detail (2-4 sentences)
3. **"This shows/exemplifies..." analysis** — connects evidence to claim
4. **Broadening sentence** (optional) — extends the point or transitions

### Introductions
Jonah opens with a broad contextual statement, then narrows to the specific topic, then states what the paper will do:

- "American democratic values emphasize the people's right to decide who governs them. Interests between groups do not always align: the poor need public services for their well-being, the rich are looking for a government that will protect their accumulated wealth, and the power hungry are looking to get a taste of political power. [...] This paper will discuss..."
- "The presidential election of 1980 was a watershed event in American political history. [...] This research paper will analyze..."

**Pattern:** Broad context → Specific framing → "This paper/essay will [discuss/analyze/look at]..." → Roadmap of sections

**Rule:** Always include an explicit "In this essay I will" or "This paper will" statement. Jonah roadmaps.

### Conclusions
Jonah starts conclusions with "In Conclusion," or "In summary," and restates the thesis in different words. Often ends with a forward-looking claim or question:

- "In Conclusion, the municipal reform movement fundamentally transformed American urban politics..."
- "What issue will this base, connected by Reagan and motivated by Conservative figureheads, tackle next?"
- "...we must reassess the reforms' long term impacts on democratic participation and also explore new waves of reforms..."

**Rule:** Start with "In Conclusion," (capitalize C, include comma). Restate, don't just summarize. End with a broader implication or call forward.

### Section Transitions
Jonah explicitly signals section transitions:

- "As mentioned above, the reform movements' goals fell under two main umbrellas..."
- "The next section will discuss the actualization of these goals..."
- "As mentioned above, the material changes that the municipal reformers were seeking fell into two categories..."

**Rule:** Use "As mentioned above" to link back. Occasionally use "The next section will" for forward signaling.

## 4. Quote and Source Integration

### Introducing Authors
Jonah introduces authors with their credentials in appositive phrases:

- "Annie Lowrey, a Harvard graduate and accomplished economic journalist, writes about..."
- "Kerryn Higgs, an Australian environmental scientist, writes about this shifting paradigm..."
- "Micael Pollan, a UC Berkely professor of journalism who focus on topics where nature and culture overlap, writes about..."
- "James Mak, professor of economics and fellow at the University of Hawai'i Economic Research Organization, writes about..."

**Rule:** First mention of an author = full name + credentials in appositive clause + "writes/argues/discusses." After first mention, use last name only.

### Quote Integration Patterns
- **"According to [Author],"** — for paraphrased or quoted claims
- **"[Author] state/argue/conclude that"** — for direct attribution
- **"As [Author] summarize,"** — before a direct quote
- **Mid-sentence embedding:** "...the adoption of city manager government had little, if any, effect on the level of city expenditures" (Hinze and Judd 2023, pg 113).

**Rule:** Jonah integrates quotes mid-sentence more than he uses block quotes. He leads into quotes with the author's argument framing, not just "X says."

### Rhetorical Questions
Jonah uses rhetorical questions to introduce analytical sections:

- "Why would reformers want to change the demographics of the electorate?"
- "Does it matter? Does the distance between us and the animals that feed us influence the ethical framework of our exploitation of them?"
- "...does she really think Thoreau is innocent or is she defending his work because she believes in his values?"
- "so what happened?"

**Rule:** Use 1-2 rhetorical questions per essay, placed at transition points to draw the reader into a new analytical thread. They should feel natural and conversational.

## 5. Word Choice and Vocabulary

### Jonah's Characteristic Words (USE THESE)
- **"mainly"** (not "primarily" or "chiefly")
- **"greatly"** — "greatly changed," "greatly hindered"
- **"heavily"** — "heavily reduced," "tilted heavily"
- **"drastically"** — "drastically reduced," "drastically more difficult"
- **"incredibly"** — "incredibly important"
- **"extremely"** — "extremely widespread," "extremely valuable"
- **"quite"** — "quite a lot," "quite sad," "quite obviously"
- **"definitely"** — "definitely worked," "definitely contributed"
- **"clearly"** — "it is clearly important," "a clear example"
- **"overall"** — "an overall poor government structure"
- **"immensely"** — "strengthens her argument immensely"
- **"a myriad of"** — "a myriad of organizations"

### Jonah's Characteristic Phrases
- **"at the expense of"** — "at the expense of broad democratic participation"
- **"hand in hand"** — "went hand in hand with progressive era ideologies"
- **"a taste of"** — "looking to get a taste of political power"
- **"in favor of"** — "tilted heavily in favor of business elites"
- **"the lifeblood of"** — "impact on the lifeblood of metropolitan areas"
- **"at the helm"** — "has the higher classes at the helm"
- **"resulting in"** — used to chain consequences mid-sentence
- **"which in turn"** — "which in turn marginalizes less influential constituencies"
- **"as a whole"** — "benefits society as a whole"
- **"double-edged sword"** — for trade-offs

### Words/Phrases to AVOID (LLM tells)
- "delve" / "delve into"
- "nuanced"
- "multifaceted"
- "tapestry"
- "landscape" (as metaphor for field/situation) — Jonah uses "landscape" but only literally or for "political landscape"
- "underscores" / "underscored"
- "sheds light on"
- "it is imperative"
- "plays a pivotal role"
- "serves as a testament"
- "a profound impact" — Jonah says "a great effect" or "a drastic impact"
- "navigating" (metaphorical)
- "robust"
- "framework" (as a vague catch-all)
- "in light of"
- "stakeholders"
- "leverage" (as a verb)
- "holistic"
- "paradigm" — Jonah uses "shifting paradigm" once but generally avoids
- "myriad" without "a" — Jonah writes "a myriad of," not bare "myriad"

## 6. Voice and Register

### Conversational-Academic Blend
Jonah writes in a voice that is academic but not stiff. He uses:
- First person: "I believe," "I think," "I will discuss," "I would like to"
- Direct assertions: "Bridges and Kronic are right when they say..."
- Casual asides: "which is interesting because," "In retrospect, this blessing is quite sad knowing that..."
- Contractions: occasionally ("didn't," "wasn't," "don't") — use sparingly, maybe 1-2 per essay
- Personal anecdotes woven into analytical writing (when the format allows)

**Rule:** The voice should feel like a smart college student who is genuinely interested in the topic and thinking through it in real time, not like a polished academic performing expertise.

### Directness Over Hedging
Jonah states positions directly:
- "I disagree" (not "one might question")
- "Bridges and Kronic are right" (not "there is merit to the argument")
- "This cannot be seen as an entirely positive facet" (not "this complicates the narrative")

**Rule:** State things. Don't hedge with "perhaps," "arguably," "one could suggest." If acknowledging complexity, use "Although" or "While" clauses, not hedging adverbs.

### "We" for Shared Analysis
Jonah occasionally uses "we" to include the reader in analysis:
- "we can see a clear increase..."
- "we will examine a change in this dynamic"
- "With this we can conclude..."

## 7. Structural Habits

### Explicit Categorization with Colons
Jonah frequently introduces lists or categories with a colon:
- "the reform movements' goals fell under two main umbrellas: changing the demographics of the electorate and reworking the structure of city government."
- "changes to the electoral process and structural changes to the municipal government."
- "These three major events were driven by a desire for capital: economic capital, political capital, and social capital."

### Pairs and Triads
Jonah naturally groups things in pairs or triads:
- "social and political," "upper and middle class," "corruption and inefficiency"
- "efficiency, transparency, and responsiveness"
- "wasted tax dollars, misappropriated funds, and an overall poor government structure"
- "formation, unification, and mobilization"

**Rule:** Use paired adjectives/nouns connected by "and." Use triads for emphasis. This is a strong Jonah fingerprint.

### Occasional Missing Commas After Introductory Clauses
Jonah sometimes omits the comma after short introductory phrases — this is natural and should NOT be "corrected":
- "To understand the changes enacted by the reform movements it is crucial to understand their motivations."
- "Pre industrialization, the political process in cities was limited..."

**Rule:** Occasionally omit the comma after short introductory phrases (1-2 times per essay). This reads as natural Jonah, not as an error.

## 8. What Jonah Does NOT Do

- Does not use em dashes (—) for parenthetical asides — use commas or "which" clauses instead
- Does not use semicolons to join independent clauses — use periods or ", and"
- Does not write very short punchy sentences for emphasis — his shortest sentences are still ~10 words
- Does not use sophisticated/rare vocabulary — keeps it accessible
- Does not over-cite (in shorter essays, minimal citations; in research papers, heavy footnotes)
- Does not start paragraphs with quotes — always leads with his own claim first
- Does not use passive voice habitually — prefers active constructions
- Does not use "one" as a pronoun ("one might argue") — uses "you," "we," or direct statements
