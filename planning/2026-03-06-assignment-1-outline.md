## Question 1: Institutions and Anarchy

**Thesis:** "Institutions mitigate the effects of anarchy but do not replace it — they operate within anarchy, not as a substitute for it."

### Intro paragraph (~75 words)
- Hook: The question of whether institutions can replace anarchy is one of the central debates in IR theory
- Stake out position: Partial agreement — institutions meaningfully reduce the negative effects of anarchy, but they do not (and cannot) serve as a substitute for central authority
- State thesis clearly
- Preview: liberal institutionalism shows mitigation; realist critique and empirical evidence show limits

### Body A: Institutions DO mitigate anarchy (~200 words)
**Claim:** Liberal institutionalism demonstrates that institutions reduce uncertainty, incentivize cooperation, and create rules that address the problems of anarchy.

Evidence to weave in:
- **[Q1-1]** Levy & Thompson: institutions "mitigate the effects of international anarchy, minimize the violence-prone tendencies of the international system" (IIP_Liberalism_Case_Study_European_Union .txt:L138-142)
- **[Q1-3]** Institutions "mitigate conflict, provide incentives to cooperate, reducing uncertainty" (IIP_Liberalism_Case_Study_European_Union .txt:L274-278)
- **[Q1-4]** Explicit institutions "function by setting the rules of the game... lowering costs of cooperation and increasing the costs of conflict" (IIP_Liberalism_Case_Study_European_Union .txt:L718-722)
- **[Q1-5]** Kant's league of peace: "maintenance and security of the freedom of the state itself" without "dominion over the power of the state" (kant-perpetual-peace.txt:L285-291)

Analysis: These mechanisms genuinely address anarchy's problems — they create predictability where there was none and make cooperation rational. The EU is the strongest case: supranational rules, enforcement via costs, and decades of peace among historically warring states.

### Body B: But institutions do NOT replace anarchy (~200 words)
**Claim:** Institutions lack the enforcement power of true central authority; power politics persist within them, and states can defect when they choose.

Evidence to weave in:
- **[Q1-7]** "Power politics remain in institutions. Institutions may provide an additional avenue through which major powers assert their influence" (IIP_Liberalism_Case_Study_European_Union .txt:L867-870)
- **[Q1-8]** States "may violate existing agreements if enforcement mechanisms are fragile" (IIP_Liberalism_Case_Study_European_Union .txt:L882-888)
- **[Q1-9]** Brexit: "UK defection demonstrates weakness of enforcement for explicit institutions" (IIP_Liberalism_Case_Study_European_Union .txt:L917-922)
- **[Q1-10]** Walt: cooperation "is always somewhat fragile" because "there is no central authority that can protect states from one another" (walt-2018-think-like-realist.txt:L99-108)
- **[Q1-11]** Hobbes: "during the time men live without a common power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called war" (hobbes-leviathan-ch13.md:L30)

Analysis: If institutions truly replaced anarchy, states could not exit. Brexit proves they can. The realist critique shows institutions are tools of the powerful, not neutral governance — the underlying logic of self-help persists.

### Body C: Synthesis — governance within anarchy (~100 words)
**Claim:** The key distinction is between governance *within* anarchy and governance *replacing* anarchy.

- **[Q1-6]** Even Kant recognized this: "a league of nations, but it would not have to be a state consisting of nations. That would be contradictory, since a state implies the relation of a superior (legislating) to an inferior (obeying)" (kant-perpetual-peace.txt:L231-240)
- **[Q1-12]** Eckstein: institutions "succeeded during the 1990s only because US dominance supported them" (eckstein-2021-anarchy.md:L42)

Analysis: Institutions represent the best humans can do short of world government — meaningful governance that depends on voluntary compliance, not sovereign authority.

### Conclusion (~75 words)
- Restate thesis in different words
- Institutions are a remarkable achievement — they make anarchy livable, not obsolete
- The question isn't whether institutions help (they do) but whether they fundamentally alter the structure of international politics (they don't)

2. Kant — perpetual peace
3. EU lecture notes — institutional mechanisms

## Question 2: Do Leaders Matter?

**Thesis:** "Leaders matter most when structural constraints are ambiguous — in crises, novel situations, and authoritarian regimes — but systemic pressures dominate when the external environment is compelling."

### Intro paragraph (~75 words)
- Hook: Whether leaders shape events or events shape leaders is a foundational question in IR
- Acknowledge the tension: both sides have strong evidence
- State thesis: leaders' influence is conditional — it depends on how constraining the external environment is
- Preview: Jervis's framework of interaction between individual and circumstance

### Body A: When leaders matter (~200 words)
**Claim:** Individual leaders have decisive influence when structural constraints leave room for choice — in crises, novel situations, and authoritarian systems.

Evidence to weave in:
- **[Q2-3]** "who leads matters" — individual beliefs create "different conceptions of foreign policy goals... different images of the adversary" (levy-thompson-causes-of-war-3.txt:L217-227)
- **[Q2-4]** Xi's diversionary aggression: "China's domestic politics compel risky behavior on the international stage" — leader's personal survival drives state behavior (carter-2019-diversionary-aggression.md:L18-20)
- **[Q2-6]** "Only Nixon could go to China" — Nixon's anti-Communist credibility enabled a policy shift no other leader could pursue (cukierman-tommasi-1998-nixon-china.txt:L60-64)
- **[Q2-7]** Afghanistan: "political considerations override intelligence assessments. The decision to withdraw was predetermined regardless of warnings" (london-2021-afghanistan-intelligence.md:L27-28)

Analysis: In each case, structural theory alone cannot explain the outcome. Xi chose diversionary aggression from a range of options. Nixon's individual identity unlocked a specific policy. Two administrations' political calculus drove Afghanistan withdrawal against intelligence warnings. The common thread: ambiguity in the external environment created space for individual agency.

### Body B: When structure dominates — counterargument (~200 words)
**Claim:** When the external environment is compelling, individual differences wash out — systemic pressures produce remarkably similar behavior across very different leaders.

Evidence to weave in:
- **[Q2-9]** Wolfers: "if the house is on fire we do not have to know anything about the individuals in it to predict that they will rush for the exits" (jervis-2013-do-leaders-matter.txt:L248-252)
- **[Q2-11]** Post-Cold War continuities: "it would be hard to infer when a new person entered the Oval Office" despite very different presidents (jervis-2013-do-leaders-matter.txt:L1122-1136)
- **[Q2-12]** Organizational process model: "The best predictor of how an organization behaves at time t is how they behaved at time t-1" (levy-thompson-causes-of-war-4.txt:L172-181)
- **[Q2-13]** "Political leaders do not implement their own policies, and there is often 'slippage'" — Truman on Eisenhower: "He'll sit here, and say 'Do this! Do that!' And nothing will happen" (levy-thompson-causes-of-war-4.txt:L710-720)

Analysis: Bureaucratic inertia, organizational routines, and systemic pressures create continuity that persists across administrations. Even when leaders want to change course, implementation slippage blunts their impact.

### Body C: The interaction — resolving the debate (~100 words)
**Claim:** The answer isn't leaders OR structure but leaders AND structure in interaction.

- **[Q2-2]** "There is an interaction between individuating and circumstantial factors" — "where one person might feel trapped another could see room for choice" (jervis-2013-do-leaders-matter.txt:L278-281)
- **[Q2-14]** Personality traits "can influence how and why leaders escalate conflict or cooperate" (IIP_Levels_of_Analysis_II.txt:L438-444)

Analysis: Jervis's insight resolves the debate — it's not about WHETHER leaders matter but WHEN. The external environment sets the range; the individual determines the specific outcome within that range.

### Conclusion (~75 words)
- Restate thesis
- The question "do leaders matter?" has no universal answer — it depends on the situation
- In ambiguous moments, leaders are decisive (Nixon, Xi, Afghanistan); in compelling environments, anyone would act similarly (Wolfers's fire)
- This conditional answer is more analytically useful than a blanket yes or no

### Source checklist: 6 distinct sources
1. Jervis (2013) — do leaders matter framework
2. Levy & Thompson Ch. 3 — individual-level analysis
3. Levy & Thompson Ch. 4 — organizational/bureaucratic constraints
4. Carter (2019) — diversionary aggression
5. Cukierman & Tommasi (1998) — credibility and leader identity
6. London (2021) — Afghanistan intelligence failure

## Question 3: Threat Perception and Ukraine

**Thesis:** "The statement reflects the constructivist insight that threats are socially constructed through identity and shared ideas, not material capability alone — and Russia's invasion of Ukraine is best understood as a response to the ideational threat posed by Ukraine's shift toward Western democratic identity and NATO membership."

### Intro paragraph (~75 words)
- Hook: The same weapon in different hands carries different meaning — this is the core of constructivism
- Directly address the prompt: the NK/UK nuclear statement illustrates that threats are constructed through identity, not material power
- State thesis: constructivism explains both the nuclear example AND Russia's invasion of Ukraine
- Preview: Part 1 = explain the principle; Part 2 = apply to Russia-Ukraine

### Body A: What the nuclear statement suggests — constructivism (~200 words)
**Claim:** The statement illustrates the constructivist principle that threats are socially constructed through identity and shared ideas, not material capability alone.

Evidence to weave in:
- **[Q3-1]** Theys/Wendt: "500 British nuclear weapons are less threatening to the United States than five North Korean nuclear weapons. The difference lies not in material weapons but in the meanings assigned to them" (theys-2018-introducing-constructivism.md:L24)
- **[Q3-2]** Lecture: "Nuclear capabilities in the United Kingdom have different meaning to the United States than nuclear capabilities in North Korea" (IIP_Constructivism.txt:L225-226)
- **[Q3-7]** Core assumptions: "Emphasizes ideational factors over material factors. Politics is historically and socially constructed (i.e., identity matters)" (IIP_Constructivism.txt:L161-163)
- **[Q3-5]** Wendt: "anarchy is what states make of it" — states interpret anarchy through the meanings they assign (theys-2018-introducing-constructivism.md:L32)
- **[Q3-8]** "States possess multiple socially constructed identities that signal their interests" (theys-2018-introducing-constructivism.md:L36)

Analysis: The UK is an ally — shared NATO identity, democratic governance, common values. North Korea is an adversary — hostile identity, opposing ideology, antagonistic history. The weapons are materially similar; the identities make them completely different threats. This is the constructivist insight: identity constitutes interest, and interest determines threat.

### Body B: Applying constructivism to Russia-Ukraine (~200 words)
**Claim:** Russia invaded Ukraine not primarily because of NATO's material expansion but because Ukraine's ideational shift toward Western democratic identity threatened Russia's identity as a great power with a sphere of influence.

Evidence to weave in:
- **[Q3-10]** "NATO and its members states signal intentions through shared identity. membership (non-membership) status may shape perceptions of states' interests" — Ukrainian identity/Russian-Ukraine context (IIP_NATO_RussiaUkraineWar.txt:L323-328)
- **[Q3-12]** "identity (allies/adversaries) implications of Ukraine's application for NATO membership — national identity (Russian/Ukrainian), status as a post-Soviet state... signaling domestic politics under democratic governance" (IIP_NATO_RussiaUkraineWar.txt:L592-597)
- **[Q3-11]** NATO as "ideological balancer" — "Russian-Ukrainian historically constructed identities — populations, cultures, languages, territories" (IIP_NATO_RussiaUkraineWar.txt:L564-568)
- **[Q3-13]** Walt: constructivist view "stress the value of incorporating [states] within the Western security community, whose members share a common identity that has made war largely unthinkable" (walt-1998-one-world-many-theories.txt:L86-96)

Analysis: Ukraine joining the Western security community would redefine Ukraine's identity from post-Soviet state in Russia's sphere to Western democracy in NATO's community. For Russia, this is not merely a military shift — it is an existential identity threat, rewriting the meaning of post-Soviet space itself.

### Body C: Why constructivism offers more than realism here (~100 words)
**Claim:** While realism identifies NATO expansion as a power shift, constructivism explains why it is perceived as threatening in the first place.

- **[Q3-14]** Security dilemma: "actions that states take to increase their security often induce a response by adversaries" (levy-thompson-causes-of-war-1.txt:L85-92) — but WHY are they seen as adversaries?
- **[Q3-16]** Realist view: "alters the power dynamics between Russia and NATO-member states" (IIP_NATO_RussiaUkraineWar.txt:L549-552)
- **[Q3-9]** "international reality isn't fixed — it remains subject to change through actors' reconceptualization of meaning and identity" (theys-2018-introducing-constructivism.md:L101)

Analysis: Realism tells us NATO expansion shifted the balance of power. But it cannot explain why Russian nukes near NATO are perceived differently than British nukes near France. Identity fills that gap — the same military action carries different meaning depending on who does it and what community they belong to.

### Conclusion (~75 words)
- Circle back to the nuclear example: same weapons, different threats, because identity determines meaning
- Russia-Ukraine follows the same logic: NATO expansion is threatening not because of tanks and missiles but because of what membership signifies — a shift in identity away from Russia's sphere
- Constructivism reveals that the international system is built on ideas as much as power
